SECTION 01

CASE OVERVIEW

⬡

DEFINITION

Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH) refers to any bleeding within the cranial vault — including the brain parenchyma, ventricles, and surrounding meningeal spaces. It constitutes a neurosurgical emergency demanding rapid radiological classification.

◈

ETIOLOGY

Primary causes include hypertensive vessel rupture, trauma, aneurysmal rupture, arteriovenous malformations (AVM), coagulopathy, and hemorrhagic transformation of ischemic infarction. Mechanism determines subtype classification.

◉

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

Extravasated blood creates a hyperdense collection on non-contrast CT within 1–3 hours. Hematoma expansion occurs in 30–40% of patients within the first 24 hours, correlating with neurological deterioration.

⬢

CLINICAL URGENCY

Mortality ranges from 35–52% at 30 days depending on GCS score, hemorrhage volume, location, and IVH extension. The 30-day "spot sign" on CTA predicts hematoma growth with high specificity.

FORENSIC ANALYSIS

TYPE I

EPIDURAL HEMATOMA

CT SHAPE Biconvex / Lenticular
BLEED SOURCE ARTERIAL (Middle Meningeal A.)
DENSITY Hyperdense (60–90 HU)
CLASSIC SIGN Lucid interval → rapid deterioration
ETIOLOGY Temporal bone fracture → arterial tear
MORTALITY RISK
~25%
TYPE II

SUBDURAL HEMATOMA

CT SHAPE Crescent / "Banana"
BLEED SOURCE VENOUS (Bridging veins)
DENSITY Acute: Hyperdense | Chronic: Hypodense
CLASSIC SIGN Midline shift, conforms to brain
ETIOLOGY Trauma, anticoagulation, elderly falls
MORTALITY RISK
~40%
TYPE III

SUBARACHNOID HEMORRHAGE

CT SHAPE Sulcal / Cisternal filling
BLEED SOURCE ARTERIAL (Aneurysm rupture)
DENSITY Hyperdense sulci & basal cisterns
CLASSIC SIGN "Thunderclap" — worst headache of life
ETIOLOGY Berry aneurysm rupture, AVM
MORTALITY RISK
~50%
TYPE IV

INTRAPARENCHYMAL

CT SHAPE Rounded, irregular hematoma
BLEED SOURCE Arterial or venous
DENSITY Hyperdense mass within parenchyma
CLASSIC SIGN Perilesional edema, mass effect
ETIOLOGY HTN, AVM, tumor, coagulopathy
MORTALITY RISK
~52%
SECTION 04

CASE CONCLUSION

FINDINGS COMPILED
01
CT remains the primary modality for rapid identification of intracranial hemorrhage in emergency settings. Non-contrast CT has near-100% sensitivity in the acute phase (<24 hours).
02
Morphological pattern recognition on CT — shape, location, density, and mass effect — is the cornerstone of forensic radiological differentiation between hemorrhage subtypes.
03
Mechanism of injury directly determines hemorrhage type: arterial pressure produces epidural/SAH; venous bridging vein rupture causes subdural; hypertension damages small perforators causing basal ganglia bleeds.
04
Machine learning classification pipelines, as demonstrated in Exhibit #04, represent the frontier of automated hemorrhage detection, with modern models achieving >90% AUC across hemorrhage categories.
05
Immediate clinical correlation with GCS, pupillary response, and hemodynamic status remains essential. Radiological evidence alone is insufficient for management decision without clinical context.
